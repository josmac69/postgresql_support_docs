OpenEverest is the new incarnation of Percona Everest — Percona's open source database-as-a-service platform. In practical terms, it provides a unified interface for provisioning and managing different types of databases on Kubernetes — currently PostgreSQL, MySQL, and MongoDB, sitting on top of Percona's Kubernetes operators. The idea is to let teams run a private DBaaS with the convenience of cloud database services (preconfigured HA, backups, disaster recovery, autoscaling) but with full control over security and data, typically as part of a platform engineering initiative.

What changed in early 2026 is the governance, not the software. Percona transitioned Everest into an independent project with open governance and a multi-vendor community, announced in January 2026. Key facts:

It keeps the same Apache 2.0 license, and the stated intention is to donate the project to the Cloud Native Computing Foundation to ensure long-term independence and vendor neutrality. A new company called Solanica was formed, fully dedicated to OpenEverest, with Sergey Pronin — who led Percona Everest for several years — deeply involved. Interestingly, Percona created a wholly-owned subsidiary to manage the project rather than donating it directly to the CNCF immediately, because the platform had expanded beyond what a single company could reasonably support. Roadmap decisions and contributions are evaluated through community processes — even a Percona competitor contributing support for a rival database would be judged on technical merit. And Percona remains an active contributor and continues to provide enterprise-class support, consulting, and services for OpenEverest users, so existing commercial customers see continuity.

The competitive context: it plays in the same space as KubeBlocks, KubeDB, and StackGres — multi-database Kubernetes control planes — and the broader "Data on Kubernetes" movement. Analysts frame it as an attempt to become a shared, vendor-neutral control plane for data infrastructure, analogous to what Kubernetes and GitOps did for compute.

From an operational and strategic standpoint, OpenEverest illustrates the industry trend toward open-source, multi-vendor database orchestration. Architecturally, OpenEverest orchestrates the Percona Operator for PostgreSQL, positioning it as a key alternative to other cloud-native operators such as CloudNativePG. A technical comparison of CNPG and Percona Operator architectures shows that while CNPG relies heavily on native Kubernetes resources and excludes an external Patroni daemon, the Percona Operator leverages Patroni for high-availability cluster consensus. OpenEverest functions above this operator layer, providing a unified control plane that simplifies multi-cluster management and cross-operator provisioning.
